# LinkedIn “About Me” Best Practices

## 1. Start with a strong first 2–3 lines

LinkedIn only shows the beginning of the About section before someone has to click “see more,” so the opening needs to quickly explain who you are, what you do, and why someone should keep reading.

**Good opening pattern:**

> Software engineer with a background in test automation, full-stack web development, and AI-assisted engineering workflows. I build reliable systems, scalable test frameworks, and practical tools that help teams ship with confidence.

Avoid openings like:

> I am a hardworking and passionate professional with a proven track record…

That sounds too generic and doesn’t tell the reader much.

## 2. Write for your target audience

Before writing, decide who the section is for: recruiters, hiring managers, engineering managers, clients, collaborators, or peers.

For a job search, the audience is usually:

**Recruiters and hiring managers who want to know what roles you fit, what technical value you bring, and whether your background matches their needs.**

## 3. Make it a professional story, not a copied resume

The About section should show personality, character, and professional context, not just a list of jobs and tools.

The About section should answer:

- **What do you do?**
- **What are you especially good at?**
- **What kind of work are you looking for?**
- **What makes your background distinct?**

## 4. Include keywords, but don’t keyword-stuff

LinkedIn profiles can be found through LinkedIn and Google search, so relevant terms matter.

For a software engineer, this might include terms like:

- Software Engineer
- Full-Stack Development
- React
- TypeScript
- Ruby on Rails
- Python
- Test Automation
- Playwright
- Performance Testing
- AWS
- Docker
- CI/CD
- AI-assisted development

The key is to work them into natural sentences rather than dumping a giant skills list.

## 5. Use concrete proof points

A strong About section should include a few specific examples of impact, not just traits.

Instead of:

> I improve software quality and collaborate cross-functionally.

Better:

> I’ve built and modernized automated test frameworks, migrated browser testing from Cucumber to Playwright, contributed to performance testing in k6, and helped teams catch reliability issues earlier in development.

## 6. Keep the tone human

The best About sections sound like a person wrote them. Use plain language. Avoid corporate filler like:

- “results-driven”
- “dynamic”
- “synergistic”
- “passionate professional”
- “proven track record”
- “fast-paced environment”

These phrases can work only when they are made specific and believable.

## 7. Make it easy to skim

A good LinkedIn About section is usually easiest to read when it uses short paragraphs and/or a short bulleted section.

A good structure:

1. Opening positioning statement
2. Brief background and strengths
3. Selected skills or impact areas
4. What you’re looking for now
5. Optional personal/professional values or working style

## 8. Be clear about what you want next

If you’re job searching, say so directly but professionally. Don’t make the reader infer it.

Example:

> I’m currently looking for software engineering roles where I can contribute to full-stack development, developer tooling, test automation architecture, or reliability-focused engineering.

This helps recruiters understand what opportunities are relevant.

## 9. Don’t over-explain a layoff

It’s okay to mention availability, but the About section should focus on your value, not the circumstances of leaving a previous job.

A short phrase like this is usually enough:

> I’m currently seeking my next software engineering role.

If needed, the layoff context can be addressed elsewhere, such as in a LinkedIn post, interview, or recruiter conversation.

## 10. Use first person

First person usually feels more natural on LinkedIn:

> I build reliable web applications and test automation systems.

Third person can sound stiff:

> Kelsey is a software engineer who builds reliable web applications…

For an About section, first person is generally warmer and more direct.

## 11. Align it with the rest of your profile

The About section and headline should work together rather than repeat each other. The headline should be concise and searchable; the About section should expand the story.

For example:

**Headline:**

> Software Engineer | Full-Stack Development | Test Automation | Python, Ruby, TypeScript, React

**About:**

> Expands on the kind of systems you’ve built, how you work, and what role you’re seeking.

## 12. End with a soft call to action

A simple closing makes it easier for people to act.

Examples:

> I’m open to software engineering opportunities and would be glad to connect with teams hiring for full-stack, backend, or reliability-focused roles.

Or:

> Feel free to reach out if you’re hiring for software engineering roles where quality, maintainability, and thoughtful automation matter.

# Recommended Formula

For a job-seeking software engineer, use this structure:

1. **Paragraph 1:** Who you are and what kind of role you’re seeking.
2. **Paragraph 2:** Your strongest technical themes and experience.
3. **Paragraph 3:** Concrete examples of impact.
4. **Paragraph 4:** Working style, values, or what makes you distinct.
5. **Final line:** Clear call to action.

# Things to Avoid

Avoid sounding like a resume pasted into paragraph form.

Avoid long, dense blocks of text.

Avoid vague claims without examples.

Avoid overusing buzzwords.

Avoid making the layoff the main story.

Avoid trying to cover every skill you’ve ever used.
