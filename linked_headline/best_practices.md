# Best Practices for Writing a LinkedIn Headline

A LinkedIn headline is the short line of text that appears directly under your name. It is one of the highest-visibility parts of your profile because it appears in search results, connection requests, comments, messages, and profile previews. LinkedIn’s own guidance says your headline does not have to be only your job title; it can explain what you do, why you do it, and what makes your work distinct.

## 1. Do not use only your current job title

Avoid headlines that are just:

- `Software Engineer`
- `Senior QA Engineer`
- `Open to Work`
- `Former Senior Software Engineer`

A title alone does not give recruiters, hiring managers, or peers enough context. A stronger headline communicates your target role, technical strengths, and professional positioning.

Better structure:

`Target Role | Key Skill Area | Key Skill Area | Value / Specialty`

Example:

`Software Engineer | Test Automation Architecture | Full-Stack Web Development | Building reliable, scalable applications`

## 2. Lead with the role you want next

Your headline should be optimized for where you are going, not only where you have been. If you are targeting software engineer roles, lead with `Software Engineer`, even if your most recent title was SDET, QA Automation Engineer, or similar.

Good:

`Software Engineer | Automation Architecture | React, TypeScript, Ruby, Python | Reliability-focused full-stack development`

Less effective:

`Senior Software Development Engineer in Test | Formerly at [Company]`

## 3. Include keywords recruiters are likely to search for

Recruiters often search by job title, skills, technologies, and domain. Career guidance sources consistently recommend using relevant industry keywords so your profile is easier to find in search.

For software engineering, useful keyword categories might include:

- Target title: `Software Engineer`, `Full-Stack Engineer`, `Backend Engineer`, `Frontend Engineer`
- Languages: `Python`, `Ruby`, `TypeScript`, `JavaScript`, `SQL`
- Frameworks/tools: `React`, `Node.js`, `Ruby on Rails`, `Playwright`, `AWS`, `Docker`
- Specialty areas: `Test Automation`, `Performance Testing`, `Web Applications`, `System Reliability`

Do not cram in every skill. Choose the terms most aligned with the roles you want.

## 4. Make the first few words count

The beginning of your headline matters most because it is what people see first in search results, comments, and previews. Put your target role and strongest positioning up front.

Strong opening:

`Software Engineer | Full-Stack Web Development...`

Weaker opening:

`Passionate problem solver and lifelong learner...`

Avoid leading with vague traits when you could lead with searchable, role-specific information.

## 5. Balance searchability with readability

A good headline should work for both LinkedIn search and human readers. It should contain relevant keywords, but still read like a professional sentence or clean phrase.

Too keyword-stuffed:

`Software Engineer | Python | Ruby | TypeScript | JavaScript | SQL | React | Node | Rails | AWS | Docker | QA | SDET`

Better:

`Software Engineer | Full-Stack Web Development | Python, Ruby, TypeScript, React | Reliability-focused engineering`

## 6. Communicate your value, not just your tools

Tools are useful, but your headline should also hint at the kind of problems you solve.

Examples of value-focused phrases:

- `Building reliable web applications`
- `Improving system quality through automation`
- `Creating scalable test and development frameworks`
- `Bridging software engineering and quality architecture`
- `Developing maintainable, user-focused applications`

A common recommendation is to combine role, skills, and value proposition rather than relying on a title alone.

## 7. Be specific, not generic

Avoid overused phrases that could apply to almost anyone.

Avoid:

- `Results-driven professional`
- `Passionate technologist`
- `Problem solver`
- `Lifelong learner`
- `Dynamic leader`
- `Seeking my next opportunity`

Use specific positioning instead:

- `Software Engineer focused on reliable web applications`
- `Full-stack developer with deep test automation experience`
- `Engineer specializing in scalable automation and application reliability`

## 8. Mention “Open to Work” carefully

If you are actively job searching, you can communicate that, but do not let it dominate the headline. The headline should still prioritize your target role and skills.

Less effective:

`Open to Work | Seeking Software Engineer Role`

Better:

`Software Engineer | Full-Stack Web Development | Python, Ruby, TypeScript, React | Open to Software Engineering Roles`

This keeps the focus on what you do and what roles you are targeting.

## 9. Avoid company-name stacking

Your headline is not the best place to list past employers. LinkedIn already shows your experience elsewhere. Use the headline for positioning, skills, and search terms.

Avoid:

`Ex-Company A | Ex-Company B | Software Engineer | QA | Automation`

Better:

`Software Engineer | Test Automation Architecture | Full-Stack Web Development | Building reliable web applications`

## 10. Keep it concise, but use the available space wisely

LinkedIn headlines are commonly treated as a short, high-impact field. Many current LinkedIn headline guides refer to a 220-character limit, so the goal is to be concise while still giving enough context.

Aim for:

- Clear enough to understand in 2–3 seconds
- Specific enough to show your niche
- Searchable enough to include target-role keywords
- Short enough to avoid looking cluttered

## 11. Use separators for readability

Common separators include:

- `|`
- `•`
- `/`
- `–`

Example:

`Software Engineer | Full-Stack Web Development | Python, Ruby, TypeScript, React | Reliability-focused engineering`

The pipe format is especially readable and easy to scan.

## 12. Match your headline to your target roles

Before finalizing your headline, compare it against the job postings you want. Ask:

- Does it include the job title I want?
- Does it include the most important technical keywords?
- Does it reflect the kind of work I want more of?
- Would a recruiter immediately understand what roles I am suited for?
- Does it align with the rest of my profile and resume?

## 13. Do not try to be too clever

Clever headlines can be memorable, but they often hurt searchability. LinkedIn is not the best place for a vague tagline unless you already have strong name recognition.

Avoid:

`Bug Slayer | Chaos Tamer | Code Whisperer`

Better:

`Software Engineer | Test Automation Architecture | Playwright, Python, TypeScript | Improving application reliability`

## 14. Update it as your job search focus changes

Your headline should not be permanent. Update it when your target role, strongest skills, or career positioning changes. LinkedIn’s own profile guidance emphasizes keeping your profile current and using the headline as more than a static title field.

## 15. Use a simple formula

A strong default formula:

`Target Role | Specialty / Domain | Key Skills | Value Proposition`

Examples:

`Software Engineer | Full-Stack Web Development | Python, Ruby, TypeScript, React | Building reliable web applications`

`Software Engineer | Automation Architecture | Playwright, Ruby, Python, TypeScript | Improving quality and reliability`

`Full-Stack Software Engineer | React, Node.js, Ruby on Rails | Test Automation & System Reliability`

`Software Engineer | Web Applications, APIs, Automation | Python, Ruby, TypeScript, SQL`

## LinkedIn Headline Checklist

Before publishing, make sure your headline:

- Starts with the role you want next
- Includes relevant recruiter-search keywords
- Highlights your strongest technical areas
- Communicates value, not just tools
- Avoids vague buzzwords
- Avoids unnecessary company names
- Is easy to scan quickly
- Matches your resume and target job postings
- Sounds like a real person wrote it
- Makes sense even to someone outside your current company
